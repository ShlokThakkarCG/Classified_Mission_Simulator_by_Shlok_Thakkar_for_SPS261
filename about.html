About This Simulation

Classified Mission Simulator is an interactive educational tool designed for a university project in course SPS261 - Government Secrecy and Intelligence Studies. It translates complex strategic trade-offs into an interactive framework, enabling users to experience the decision-making pressures that define contemporary space governance.

Grounded in Academic Research

This simulation is not an arbitrary game; its design is directly derived from the foundational academic papers provided for this university project. The ten-mission structure and four-metric evaluation system operationalize these theories into a playable simulation, translating academic models into experiential learning.

The core logic of the simulation is built by synthesizing two key works:

  • Vaynman and Volpe (2023), "Dual Use Deception: How Technology Shapes Cooperation in International Relations" DOI: https://doi.org/10.1017/S0020818323000140
  • Zagare (2019), "Game Theory, Diplomatic History and Security Studies" DOI: https://doi.org/10.1093/oso/9780198831587.001.0001

This page details how those concepts were used to build the simulation's logic.

Core Concepts & Methodology

The simulation is built on a framework of competing strategic priorities. Each decision forces the user to weigh the costs and benefits associated with secrecy, security, diplomacy, and public perception.

1. The Vaynman & Volpe "Dead Zone"

Vaynman & Volpe (2023) argue that cooperation on technology is determined by two factors: distinguishability (can you tell civilian and military uses apart?) and integration (is the tech embedded in both sectors?).

This simulation is set in what they term the "dead zone" of cooperation: assets that are both indistinguishable and highly integrated. Space systems, like AI and cyber, fit this perfectly. A satellite's sensor array can be used for climate science (civilian) or for targeting (military), and the ground stations are integrated with both civilian and military networks. This is the core problem: deception is easy, and verification is nearly impossible without risking espionage.

2. Zagare's Game-Theoretic Modeling

Zagare (2019) argues that game theory provides a rigorous way to model historical crises. This simulation adopts that method. Each of the 10 missions is a "game" in the formal sense—a strategic interaction where the player must choose a move without knowing the opponent's full response. The player's decisions are moves in an Asymmetric Escalation Game or a Game of Chicken, much like those Zagare uses to analyze the Cuban Missile Crisis or the "Long Peace."

3. The Four Metrics (The "Payoff" Structure)

In Zagare's game-theoretic models, each choice has a "payoff." In this simulation, the payoffs are quantified by the four key metrics. Every decision is a trade-off, and the "payoff" is the change in these four variables, which represent the competing priorities of a national leader:

  • Public Trust: Represents the public's and media's confidence in your program. High trust is essential for securing funding and maintaining political support, but it is highly sensitive to perceived deception or failure.
  • Budget: The financial resources available to your program. It is depleted by costly maneuvers and compensation, and can be increased through political victories or allied contributions.
  • System Resilience: The technical and operational integrity of your space assets. This is increased by protecting secrets and neutralizing threats, but decreased by sharing technology, suffering breaches, or revealing capabilities.
  • Diplomatic Stability: Represents the state of relations with other space-faring nations, including allies and rivals. It is improved through transparency and cooperation, but damaged by unilateral actions, deception, and aggressive posturing.

Mission-by-Mission Breakdown

Each scenario in the simulation is a practical case study designed to test one or more of the core academic concepts. Here is how each mission connects the theory to the required decisions:

Mission 1: The Argus-1 Launch

Concepts Applied: Vaynman & Volpe's Framework (Distinguishability).

This mission is a direct test of "distinguishability." The "Climate Research" cover story is an attempt to make a dual-use asset seem civilian (high distinguishability). The "Debris Monitor" is more honest but admits a military-adjacent function. The choice immediately impacts Diplomatic Stability, as rivals must guess your true intent in the "dead zone."

Mission 2: Unidentified Object

Concepts Applied: Zagare's Game Theory (Risk & Caution).

A simple game of risk. Maneuvering (Caution) has a small, certain cost (Budget) but guarantees asset safety (System Resilience). "Pinging" the object is a gamble: it may reveal information, but as Vaynman & Volpe's work implies, it also risks revealing your capabilities (lowering System Resilience) by exposing your "digital signature."

Mission 3: The Anomaly

Concepts Applied: Vaynman & Volpe (Integration).

This mission explores the "integration" problem. The flaw is not just a technical problem; it's a public relations one because the satellite is integrated with public life. Admitting the flaw (high Public Trust) risks exposing a vulnerability (low System Resilience). A media blackout is an attempt to hide the flaw, but it fails, proving that trust is a critical component of policy in the "dead zone."

Mission 4: Allied Tech Request

Concepts Applied: Vaynman & Volpe (Distinguishability & Integration).

A classic "disclosure constraint." Sharing technology improves Diplomatic Stability and Budget, but it is an irreversible act of proliferation. Because the tech is indistinguishable and integrated, you have permanently reduced your System Resilience by giving away a core secret. This is the high-risk, high-reward nature of cooperation in the "dead zone."

Mission 5: The 'Shadow'

Concepts Applied: Zagare's Asymmetric Escalation Game.

This is a "game of chicken," as analyzed by Zagare. A rival is escalating. Your choices are clear "moves": 1) Protest (de-escalate, lose face), 2) Deniable EW (match escalation, high risk), 3) "Dazzle" (aggressive escalation, high reward). The player must decide if protecting System Resilience is worth the severe Diplomatic Stability cost of "swerving" last.

Mission 6: Budget Committee Threat

Concepts Applied: Vaynman & Volpe (Distinguishability).

This mission links Public Trust directly to Budget. To justify funding, you must choose between declassifying a minor capability (making it "distinguishable" as a public good) or holding firm. The PR win boosts Budget but, per Vaynman & Volpe, any disclosure (even minor) lowers System Resilience by revealing a piece of the puzzle.

Mission 7: Debris Collision

Concepts Applied: Zagare's Game Theory (Payoff Structure).

This models a scenario with a "dominant losing strategy." Attempting plausible deniability is designed to fail, demonstrating that in a game with perfect information (the public will find out), a failed cover-up has a worse payoff (catastrophic Public Trust loss) than simply taking the initial, costly hit to Budget.

Mission 8: Ground Station Breach

Concepts Applied: Vaynman & Volpe (Integration).

This is the "integration" problem in action. The civilian and military networks are one. You must choose what to sacrifice: System Resilience (by not patching the vulnerability) or Public Trust (by patching it, but letting the data leak). It's a "no-win" scenario created by high integration, just as the paper predicts.

Mission 9: Rival ASAT Test

Concepts Applied: Zagare's Perfect Deterrence Theory.

This models a major "red line" event that fundamentally changes the game's state. The rival's test has broken deterrence. Condemning the act is a diplomatic move that fails to solve the physical threat (System Resilience drops). Announcing your own ASAT program is a classic move in deterrence theory—re-establishing equilibrium through mutual threat, but at a catastrophic cost to Diplomatic Stability and Budget.

Mission 10: Hostile Jamming

Concepts Applied: Vaynman & Volpe's "Dead Zone" & Zagare's Escalation.

The final "game." Faced with an act of war, the player must make their last move. The diplomatic option (sharing data) follows international norms but is a "loss." The military option ("activate counter-measure") proves Vaynman & Volpe's theory: the satellite was always an indistinguishable weapon. This act wins the immediate battle (high System Resilience) but proves the worst fears of rivals and the public, causing a total collapse in Diplomatic Stability and Public Trust—the exact "disaster" outcome that the "dead zone" framework predicts.

Design Philosophy

The interface and feedback system of the Classified Mission Simulator were intentionally designed to reflect the aesthetics of bureaucratic control centers—minimalist, data-driven, and emotionally neutral. This design choice reinforces the project’s thematic intent: decisions made under secrecy appear rational and procedural, even when their long-term outcomes are destabilizing. The player’s experience of tension between clarity and opacity mirrors the institutional paradox at the core of modern state secrecy.

Project Purpose

The objective is not to "win" in a traditional sense, but to force the user to experience the consequences of their choices. The simulation uses Zagare's game-theoretic method to model the real-world policy dilemmas created by the "indistinguishable and integrated" technologies described by Vaynman & Volpe. By the end, the player experiences how a series of small, rational decisions can cascade into systemic instability—or how careful, balanced policy can navigate the "dead zone" of modern space governance.

I built this simulator during my course SPS261 Government Secrecy and Intelligence Studies — a class about how power and information shape what the public is allowed to see.

Why I Made This

When I watched Johnny Harris’s Space Wars video, something clicked. The video wasn’t just about satellites — it was about how countries hide their intentions behind perfect public images. That tension between what’s visible and what’s true reminded me of our course discussions on secrecy and intelligence. I wanted to build something that lets people sit with that feeling, not just analyze it.

What I Learned

Building this showed me secrecy isn’t only about hiding — it’s about balancing risks, timing, and trust. Sometimes it protects. Sometimes it isolates. Most of the time, it’s both.
